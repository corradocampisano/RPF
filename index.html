After the news about the cosmic gold mine was out, I could not retain from having enough of this mess and have my say about it.

Since black holes are predicted by General Relativity, I had little to say about the previous detections of gravitational waves, since gravitational waves too are predicted by General Relativity.

Yes of course I'd have asked "what's vibrating?" and I'd have got very little satisfaction from a "spacetime itself" answer, but that's for later.

For now let's focus on the fact that if you restrict to GR, neutron stars shouldn't exist in the first place: how could they generate gravitational waves, then?

Looks like GR has to "borrow" the Pauli exclusion principle from Quantum Mechanics, in order for the neutron degeneracy (a quantum phenomenon) to provide the pressure needed to prevent the star from collapsing under gravitation.

That's not unique, as the same applies to white dwarfs: they should not exists, if restricting to GR only: the exclusion principle is yet again needed to provide electron degeneracy (another quantum phenomenon), to provide the pressure needed.

Not to mention that GR has little to say about regular stars too: yes gravity makes the gas nebula to collapse into a pre-star, but you need QDC (who knows nothing about gravity and how the pre-star formed) to understand how stars ignite and burn their fuel.

The astonishing realization hits you when you understand that Quantum Mechanics has nothing to do with Continuum Mechanics, of which GR is just an instance, the one describing our own implementation of spacetime metrics.

In GR, you have a metric tensor and a mass density in each point of spacetime, while in QM you have a complex number to represent a particle's state: they "speak different mathematics", but it's not just that.

GR and QM "speak different physics" too: gravitation is on one side, the other 3 forces on the other side, photons are shared, but mass is not (or not completely): in QM it's only inertial mass, no support for gravitation, it's too weak, sorry.

I have had enough of this mess, and it's not just me, there's plenty of physicists who don't like this status quo:

  • Carlo Rovelli: he wrote similar "GR vs QM" arguments in his books and he's proposing LQG as a new way.
  • Leonard Susskind: he honestly admitted supersymmetry and string theory failures, let alone when he mentioned SR was "continuum mechanics really"
  • Lee Smolin: just read The trouble with Physics to get an idea
  • Frank Wilztcek: he got a nobel first, but recently must have gone mad: he basically said crazy things, quite like the ones I wrote 9 months earlier, about Einstein and the ether.
  • Richard P. Feynmann: he looked happy while saying that "nobody understands QM", while here (check his body language @0:43), looks like he's sitting on a cactus, after being asked a "perfectly reasonable question".

So, this GitHub repo is a call for a collective effort in refactoring the foundations for Physics: the "experimental behaviour" will stay the same, and so the restricted validity of current theories, but we'll try to get a meaningful conceptual framework, out of this messy theorethical patchwork.

At the moment it's just a set of web pages, images and videos for talking about Physics: at least that's what the web had been invented for, right?

It's about Physics, but it will by an IT perspective, so anybody on GitHub can join, as long as they survive either the complete Theoretical Minimum or this shorter Contemporary Physics Recap by Rovelli.
